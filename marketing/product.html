Baseline calibration comes first

The first month builds the foundation for accurate risk detection

1

First month builds the reference model

SignalTrue observes your team's normal work rhythm across 6 full weeks. This establishes baseline patterns for recovery time, meeting load, response norms, and collaboration distribution.

2

No recommendations during calibration

You won't see risk signals during the baseline period. This prevents false alarms and ensures accuracy. The system is learning what "normal" means for your organization.

Why this matters: Without a stable baseline, every metric is just a number. With calibration, you see when patterns are drifting from what's sustainable for your organization.

Five early warning signals

Each signal follows the same structure: what changes, why it matters, what leaders can do

Signal Type

Recovery Gap Index

What changes

Hours between last activity and first activity next day. When recovery windows shrink consistently, exhaustion risk increases.

Why it matters

Sustained recovery loss reduces cognitive resilience. Teams with shrinking recovery gaps show higher rates of errors, rework, and disengagement within 2-5 weeks.

What leaders can do

  • Enforce quiet hours for messages and meetings
  • Remove or shift late recurring meetings
  • Introduce protected recovery policy on critical days
Signal Type

Focus Fragmentation Score

What changes

Percentage of days with at least one 90-minute uninterrupted block. Loss of focus time signals cognitive load increase and execution capacity loss.

Why it matters

Deep work requires sustained attention. When fragmentation increases, decision quality declines and throughput slows. Expect delays and rework within 1-3 weeks.

What leaders can do

  • Introduce two no-meeting blocks per week
  • Collapse recurring meetings. Remove lowest-value 1‚Äì2
  • Set meeting default to 25/50 minutes to create gaps
Signal Type

Meeting Load Drift

What changes

Sustained upward trend in meeting hours per person over 4+ weeks. Meeting creep reduces autonomy and available execution time.

Why it matters

Incremental meeting additions compound quickly. Teams experiencing sustained meeting drift show reduced throughput and increased execution delays within 3-5 weeks.

What leaders can do

  • Remove 1‚Äì2 low-impact recurring meetings
  • Cap attendee count. Require agenda for >=8 attendees
  • Convert status meetings to async updates
Signal Type

Responsiveness Pressure Index

What changes

Median response time decreasing and response variance tightening. Indicates escalating reactivity norms and urgency creep.

Why it matters

Tightening response expectations reduce strategic thinking capacity. High reactivity pressure correlates with burnout risk and shallow decision-making within 2-5 weeks.

What leaders can do

  • Define response-time expectations by channel
  • Create async update cadence for non-urgent work
  • Protect focus blocks. Disable notifications during blocks
Signal Type

Participation Imbalance

What changes

Top 20% of team members carrying increasing share of collaboration activity. Participation concentration often precedes disengagement.

Why it matters

Sustained imbalance creates burnout risk for high contributors and withdrawal risk for others. Team friction and disengagement often appear within 3-8 weeks.

What leaders can do

  • Rotate ownership for recurring meetings and updates
  • Reduce meeting attendance sprawl. Clarify owners
  • Audit workload distribution and decision rights

How benchmarking works

Default: Self-comparison

SignalTrue benchmarks teams against their own historical baseline by default. You see deviation from your organization's normal patterns, not industry averages.

This prevents false positives from comparing fast-moving startups to established enterprises, or distributed teams to co-located ones.

Optional: External context

External benchmarks are available as optional context, not performance scores. They help answer "Is this unusual for teams like ours?" but never replace self-comparison as the primary signal.

External benchmarks are role-based (engineering, sales, support) and size-adjusted (8-15, 16-30, 31+ members).

Privacy by design

How aggregation works and why it protects trust

What data is used

  • Calendar event metadata (start/end times, attendee count, organizer flag)
  • Slack message metadata (timestamp, channel type, thread structure)
  • No message content, no file contents, no individual work artifacts

What is never used

  • Message or email content
  • File contents or document text
  • Keystroke or screen activity
  • Individual productivity scores

How aggregation works

  • All signals computed at team level (minimum 8 active members)
  • Individual data never shown to managers or leaders
  • Patterns require 60% data coverage to prevent sparse-data leakage

Why this protects trust

  • Leaders see system issues, not individual behavior
  • No incentive for micromanagement or surveillance
  • Teams understand the system detects overload, not poor performance
